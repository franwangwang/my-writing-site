I am now a week back into the default world, sitting on a sun bed on the top deck of a liveaboard in the Sea of Cortez, Mexico. I'd just booked this trip three days before departing, which is story for another time. This is one of the more luxurious liveaboards that I've been on, which contracts dramatically with the days and nights at Burning Man, just a week ago.

I had learned about Burning Man from the media and a couple acquaintances who went in the past - you hear about the horrible storm and people getting stuck, the sex parties, the drugs, the rich VC bros and Silicon Valley founders, etc., none of those resonated with me. Then in January this year when meeting An for the first time, he told me about Burning Man - the vulnerability, authenticity and generosity he felt from the community, the crazy talent that artists of all kinds showcase, and how he felt that everyone can be themselves. He was clearly excited and felt bad for keep talking about Burning Man, but it particularly spoke to me on that day, when I just had a major breakdown earlier in the day about my marriage. I felt that if I were ever going to Burning Man, this was the year. So I asked him right then if he can take me.

We talked about that moment on Day 5 on the playa, I asked him if he thought I was serious when I said I wanted to go. He said he felt my intention was real, but it's just one of those "let's go skiing" comments, and people don't follow up and there goes another season. Little did he know how dead serious I was back then, then March came around and I finally gathered up all my coverage to put an end to things, I sensed that my first Burning Man would be very meaningful. And here I am reporting back that it indeed was.

## Everything here, someone brought it in and built it

Serious burners would probably feel offended or at least a little frustrated to hear people comparing Burning Man to a festival, and it is truly, fundamentally different. Sure there are similarities - music, drugs and friends, but that's about it. You can pay up at a festival for VIP entrance, fancy lodging and transportation, but at Burning Man, a couple weeks before and a couple weeks after the Burn, it's just a flat desert with absolutely nothing in it except sands. An insisted on doing my first burn the right way, which is joining a camp, help with build, and sleep in a tent - no shower, no AC, no Internet (in fact we put our phones on airplane mode the moment we arrived at the playa until we drove up). It felt freeing.

Our camp was tiny, fewer than 20 people, so our build was rather simple - a 260 sq ft shade structure. On that very hot Sunday afternoon after most campmates arrived, I learned all about drilling lag bolts into the ground, using bungee balls to tie the tarps to the EMT poles, ratcheting the structure down to the ground, etc. Having participated in build definitely put things in perspectives for me seeing some camps' crazy elaborate build and those large scale delicate art installations out on the playa. Everything we see here, someone brought it in and built it by hand, piece by piece.

## "The playa will provide"

That was one of my favorite sayings at Burning Man, and the playa does provide. Each camp has their unique interactivities, every individual has their unique offering to the community. Our camp was the Sovereign Nation of Farkovia, the smallest micro nation in the world with a population of 10+ people. We had an immigration booth, a welcome center with information of our nation and its citizens, a national park that's made up of a 1ft x 1ft artificial lawn and a stuffed capybara, and a bus stop sign (which I built with campmate Kathy). We issued passports to visitors that come to our country, we'd chat with them to learn a bit about them and then grant them tourist visa under a new name that we gave them. That words play part was always hard for me, but we have some very creative campmates who carried the party through.

Our concept and interactivity was definitely silly. There is a wide variety of offerings by different camps. We were placed at 9:45 & E - navigation is straightforward in the City, it starts at 2 o'clock and ends at 10 o'clock, the center area right in the middle of the clock is called the Esplanade, then beyond that it's called the playa all the way until the trash fence, which is a fence that marks the boundary of Burning Man where police awaits on the other side if you bypass the fence, one of the few but strictly enforced rules at Burning Man. 9:45 & E was surrounded by the sound camps, and everyone knew it that whenever hearing our address, people felt bad for us. Our neighbor camp from across the street is called Misto, which had a beautiful dome with impressive sound system, so impressive that the best earplugs can block out most of the music but the bass vibrating through the ground to our cots / air mattress / RV beds was simply unstoppable. They were an interesting neighbor - they blasted EDM 24/7 for the first three days that killed our whole camp's sleep (and probably theirs too), most of the time it's just the DJ himself and one or two people there. The DJ looked like a Ukrainian mafia, but when we talked to him and gave him a polaroid of him DJing, he gave a cute bear hug. They went so hard early in the week that literally died for the last two main days, which tends to be when people go the hardest. They also serve borscht (soup) and varenyky (dumplings) every late afternoon, and there would be long lines out on the street. An and I saw the chef sitting on the beanbag in our welcome center one night looking burned out (man literally served hundreds of people every day), he said we should try his borscht. He did come through and let us skip the line the next day - the soup was very hearty.

There is such a large variety of camps and I only visited a tiny fraction - reasons to go back for more, I'll briefly list out some:

A lot of bars with unique concepts: I sang karaoke at the Hotel Lobby, which had you go through a check in process where they asked if you had any emotional baggage to check in. Hardly's was doing a naked happy hour when I walked in, and the bar tenders were suspiciously blending cheap whiskey, Reese's peanut butter cups, coconut water, whipped cream and ice, it was so unreasonably good that I'm putting Hardly's at the top of the bar list on Beli.

Physical activities oriented camps: arial (I learned how to climb silk from Kathy), monkey bars / rings (which An absolutely crushed), coconut wrestling (which Matt in our camp won), etc. Gymnasium is one of the workout camps that set up outdoors workout equipment including barbells, and every time I passed it, someone was actually working out under the scorching sun. They host a naked beer mile run every year, where you chug a beer, run 1/4 mile, then repeat 3 times. An did this last year - he was leading by a huge margin in the first 1/4 mile because he chugged the beer while others were all swirling the can, then as he ran, he realized why they were swirling - all the gas came up and he ended up being fourth to last out of 20+ people. He wanted to go back to redeem himself this year, so we went. I'll tell you more about this later.

Shower and sauna camps were much so appreciated. We went to the foam camp, which was a super impressive dome build where everyone got naked and first walked under one station where campers dumped foam from the second floor. That was day 4 since arriving at Burning Man, I felt so happy being able to release my French braid and properly shampoo my hair. Then everyone moved to the next station where they showered us with water hoses from above. It was so refreshing. Better yet, the next day we got introduced to a sauna camp right after doing the beer mile run, it was a small room that snugly fit about ten people (of course everyone naked again). Super hot steam came out and we would spray each other with hot water to rinse off. Even if there was no soap, I felt even cleaner compared to the foam shower, probably because all the pores got cleaned. It was so hot that after sitting in the sauna room for 10 minutes the outside felt chilly.

Food and drinks camps: I guess when people focus on the one thing they do really well, it really shows. On Thursday of the week, we had decided to take the day off and went to bed at 9pm, which was glorious. On Friday we woke up at 7am, determined to explore the other side of the city and collect as many breakfast food items as we can. So we had a fluffy pancake donut right outside our camp as we started the morning, avocado toasts from two camps, naked DIY bacon, a mac & cheese breakfast drink (which is not as gross as it sounds), various delicious tea and alcoholic drinks, DIY French press coffee at Cafe du Monde + dim sum (they forgot the steamer and used the crawfish giant pod instead, genius).

Bondage, speed dating, sex parties: I only explored 1% of all the possibilities here, more on this later.

Meditation, yoga, healing, messages, facials, etc.: There was a pussy erotic massage that I was very interested in, but they were already all booked out for the week when I went to the camp on Tuesday.

Stands at deep playa: The area closer to the trash fence is called deep playa, where we spent at least half the night biking and exploring, always new surprises. Some of my most memorable interactivities were all in deep playa, another more on this later.

## Music and art of all forms

Chasing big name DJs was not the point here / there are much better events to do that at, but there was definitely music playing 24/7, from the sound camps in our neighborhood, to the bars or anyone with a speaker really in the City, to every direction on the Esplanade. On the Esplanade, there were dozens of stages of varying sizes, and then a couple dozens more art carts that also blast music as they roam around. Art carts are moving structures on wheels usually with sound systems. So imagine at any given point you have 50+ live sets to choose from, or rather stumble upon, since vast majority of the sets are not published with no set locations. Techno definitely was the mainstream here, there were several big DJ sets including Rufus, Diplo, Tokimonsta, Tycho, etc., and lots of smaller DJs. We went to watch our DJ friend Zuco play one night, and I honestly loved it more than most of the big name DJs I heard that week. There was also live singing and instruments involved sometimes, including one hazy morning after a long all nighter magical trip, I woke up to live keyboards and electric guitar playing over some tracks. We went outside our tent and saw an older couple who lived in the RV next to us pulled out their setup and doing a jam, we stood there and enjoyed our private concert for a while.

And then of course there's the art. There were countless art installations out on the Esplanade, I'll just share several that left a special place in my heart.

**The Frog:** this was the first larger scale installation that I ran into in the week after a long day of driving into Burning Man. It was this giant and adorable wooden frog, where you can go inside the belly and the artist explained that he's from Puerto Rico where the ecosystem has been degraded by the day, for a call of awareness for climate change in his hometown. You can hear the frog sounds from inside, very peaceful. We were doing a quick scan so we left very quickly but I wanted to come back to the frog especially during our vitamin trips, sadly I never found it again.

**The sunken ship and dragon:** this was almost 1:1 real life size installation where two ends of the shipwreck were above the ground. You can walk in the tilted shipwreck, although interestingly once you are inside it feels almost level. Inside you can read sailors' handwritten notes, and check out their wardrobe. On the side of the shipwreck was a majestic dragon. We had decided that it was a friendly dragon, and to me, a female dragon, and there she was, swimming next to the sunken boat and blowing out magical bubbles, and her body going in and out of the playa sands / ocean. Standing close to her body, we can see the individual pieces of her skin and the lights underneath. The meticulous and intentional details were shocking. The whole time there was mystic music playing, I thought it was part of the installations and said it's so fitting, only to be pointed out that the music came from an art cart parked next to the dragon.

**The heart in a cage:** we saw it on our first scan of the playa, and I'm so glad I snapped a picture of it back then, because I was in no shape or form capable to carry my phone with me when we actually explored it again, well into our L trip, and it's a little hard to explain unless you see it. We climbed onto the cage and through the shaking the ladder, into the heart. It was pretty snug in there, a few more girls climbed in from the other side and joined us. There were prompts inside the heart and one of the girls did a heart warming reading. Honestly I barely recall what it was about, but I remembered the warm and fuzzy feeling vividly and I hugged my campmates tight.

## Nudity, sex parties...

Of course those all exist, but for me in a liberating way (obviously self selected which ones I visited). Before Burning Man, I wasn't so comfortable with nudity, at least in front of the opposite gender (thank you Japanese onsen for making me pretty comfortable being nude in front of ladies). Not that I'm not confident with my body, I just felt self conscious. At Burning Man you would see that as the week progressed, people started wearing less and less clothing / gave less and less fuck. I had been completely naked for various occasions during Burning Man (yes default is in front of both gals and dudes, and everyone still wore boots because that's for safety reasons) - the foam shower (where we against all odds ran into our friend Zuco in line and thus learned about his DJ set later in the week), the sauna rooms, naked bar / grills, etc., but the moment that I truly broke free was during the naked beer mile run.

I'm pretty good at cardio, and terrible at drinking beer, which I learned during Oktoberfest not being able to get drunk because I was too bloated before getting tipsy. The race setup is you start with chugging one beer, then you take off. An and I made the mistake of eating an hour before heading to the race, so even the first beer was hard. That first run was when the magic happened for me. I was last to finish chugging the beer vs the other men, so I was last on the race track. The sun was burning with no clouds in the sky, but the moment I started running and felt the wind on my face, I felt so liberated. I thought for a split of a second that unlike all the other naked events, I was the only girl naked running on the street, but random burners started cheering for me "you go get them girl!", which was exactly the boost of encouragement I needed to pick up the speed, despite feeling all the gas coming up my stomach.

The second beer was the hardest for me. One of the male participants opted for drinking the same amount of water, and as I was trying to chug the second beer, I told An that I'll probably switch to water for the rest of the race. The second 1/4 mile though was somehow easier, I started gunning for it. By the time I went back to the beer station, a few guys joined us. They finished the beer a bit earlier than I did, but I bypassed them pretty quickly and hearing "damn she's fast!" made me feel good. I went back to station, that was the point I realized I lost my pasties. One of the guys I surpassed soon handed them back to me "you were going so fast your pasties fell off!" This happened again and again for the rest of the runs, each time a different man picked them up for me, including the last bonus run off when I was leading the race but slipped on sand while sprinting around a corner and ate shit. The enormous bruises I had on my knees brought everyone who saw them great concern. They were indeed hurting like crazy a whole week after the race, so bad that I had to go see a doctor to see if I accidentally broke anything and can still book this dive trip. Thankfully the doctor gave me the clear, but was confused how I got hurt so bad during a running race. Proud to say I won the race against all other male participants including An, but a lady from Colorado beat all of us by a huge margin. She's a marathon runner and really good at drinking, and competes in the naked beer mile every year. "If you are good at something, you should just keep doing it." I loved that.

Then of course orgy camp - yes it was low-key on my bucket list because I'm intrigued, and I'm glad I experienced it. There are some rules, for starters, you can only get in as a couple or more-somes. The space is divided into three sections, first section is where you can only play with your partners, second section is where you are free to ask another person to play (but consent is required), third section is exhibitionists. You are allowed to look around but not stare for a long time. There are mattresses set up on the floor, and they provide condoms, lube, wipes, etc. I felt pretty safe. A few takeaways:

Takeaway 1: I went during the day, and it was way too hot to actually enjoy sex, despite their attempt at providing AC.

Takeaway 2: Most people's sex positions are pretty vanilla.

Takeaway 3: Witnessing orgy was less erotic than I had imagined, my impression was just couples enjoying each other. There are many other camps for much crazier sexual experiences though.

## Spirituality

There were two major highlights for me during the week. One being visiting the temple. Temple and the man were built by the Burning Man organizers. An had told me that it's always a very powerful experience for him and that I should visit solo. I didn't understand his words until I went.

Starting from the wooden frames that hold up the gate of the temple, you see people's writing about their losses - any loss, from their loved ones, to their pets, to their body, etc. I started tearing up right there before even entering. I was touched by the words, the strong emotions, and the impact that some of these people had on the communities around them. A memorable one was about a burner, the note said that he spread the Burning Man passion and spirit to the community, and had an audio player that you can press. You hear this very vivid voice of a middle age sounding man hollering at people to join karaoke night.

I slowly made my way inside the temple, and noticed many solo temple visitors quietly shedding tears and mourning. At that time, it still wasn't clear to me how this experience would relate to me, who I would be grieving for - I've had my fair share of losing people since I was a child, but nothing was obvious at that moment. But as I continued reading the messages on every single wall space, I realized it wasn't a person that I'm mourning, it was a thing - my seven year journey with my ex / five year marriage coming to an end. It was Thursday of the Burn, and all the space was already filled up. It took me a while to find a small empty space on top of a flight of a few stairs, a man looking like in his forties was sitting on the stairs having his moment. I started writing, and it was clear to me when I raised my hand that I would address this to my ex. I used the nickname we had for each other, something we've stopped using since the breakup. I thanked him for being the constant in my turbulent twenties, and for giving me the space to let me grow into the person I am today, even if it meant that our journey together had to end. I told him that I'll go on and live my own life now, without him physically but part of him and us will forever be with me. The last seven years came flashing by me, so many emotions came up - love, loss, resentment, guilt, bitter sweet, gratitude, etc., and only tears came out, no more words.

I sat on the stairs for a while after writing the message. The rest of the crew must have been outside waiting, so after my tears stopped coming down I gathered myself and got up. It was past sunset and the lights started lighting up the space - it was holy, and beautiful.

There was a picture of me looking into the temple. That one last gaze gave me some kind of closure. We didn't stay for the temple burn to beat the traffic, but knowing that in exactly three days the message will be sent into the sky gave me a sense of groundedness.

On the Wednesday evening after the Burn, our camp had a decompression party at Dolores Park, and we went around sharing our highlights and lowlights. Pastor Mingle shared this story that involved officiating for an uncommitment ceremony where the couple had both been divorced and traumatized from their prior marriages, and the ceremony vows went something like "I would never ever ever ever marry you, but I want to choose you every single day." Tears rolled down my face hearing that - it resonated so much that I would never want to marry for security or formality, and I'll never take my partner for granted.

## Vitamins

Alcohol was as common as water here, especially at our camp. I got used to seeing the Aussies (in particular the Radams + Pete combo) in our camp holding a beer at 10am. I was determined to go on an all vitamin diet so barely drank except as part of interactivities, and let's just say that during the week I had everything I had ever tried + everything I ever wanted to try and more, at least seven / eight kinds. It was also helpful that Radams was a walking pharmacy, I was so impressed that his nose was still functioning with all the vitamins consumption and playa dust.

L was one of the few bucket list items I had for the week, the other highlight of the week alongside the temple visit. Earlier in the week I had listened to an Asian guy giving a talk at the Center Camp on psychedelic experiences, and how you can't put into words the feelings of such trips. I 100% agree. The next day we woke up and tried to write down everything we remembered in chronological order, and it was seven pages long on iPhone. I'll just document a few things here and there, even though it won't do the trip justice.

We took the tabs around 6pm, right before the Rufus sunset set. An and I would have to miss Portola this year because our friend is getting married that weekend in Italy, so seeing him in flesh brought us so much joy. Sunset was a magical time on the playa, and that was my favorite set during the week. At some point I had to pee, so Pete, Matt, An and I went on this long journey to the port-a-potties, during which the trip started for me, so curious and distracted by everything - in hindsight I was lucky that I didn't pee my pants. After that it was getting chilly, so we biked back to camp to get our fur coats. Everyone was at camp, which made me happy. But I had some water bladder leakage issue, which was way too hard for all of us in that state of mind to fix, so we ended up getting a new one from Chris our camp lead. By the time all that got sorted we had lost everyone, and it was just Matt, An and myself. I felt bad that my water bladder took so long to fix that Leah got tired and decided to stay back, and wish we had the whole crew with us, but An said he had a feeling that it was just going to be the three of us at the end anyways.

We biked into deep playa - we had all seen the light rings installations, that night it was really calling us (so was everything else). We biked through the rings which ends at an art cart playing an awesome DJ set, and the lights felt like they were changing colors and patterns with the music. We parked our bikes and started dancing our asses off, Matt was such an amazing dancer that I thought he had danced more seriously before. He told me later that "nope, the key to dancing is you listen to the music and your heart, and dance like you don't give a fuck about what anyone else thinks." At some point a techno rat showed up. I really wanted to talk to them, but at that point of the night I was feeling mute, so I just danced next to them and admired. We danced so hard that by the time we decided we should go, someone said we killed it on the dance floor.

At some point An saw some lights and wondered if it was a bouncy castle. We biked over and it was the same bouncy castle that An spent the whole night in, also on an L trip, two Burns ago!! They had named her Emily, because of the letter E looking structure on the ceiling. We climbed inside and An tried to flip Emily, we almost succeeded until Matt was collapsing on me that I could barely breathe. We just cuddled there for a while.

We came back to Emily for sunrise. I decided to take off on a solo ride for a bit, and met up with Matt and An when I felt the sun was almost up. Emily was pretty crowded, but I joined them in the corner leaning into the net facing the sunrise. I guess my fat ass tilted the balance and we almost flipped Emily, except this time not intentionally. It was hilarious and we were laughing uncontrollably like hyenas, but we also couldn't see the sunrise. There was another couple left in Emily at that point, just staring at us, not amused at all.

I eventually managed to get up with my strong core (and An's strong arm's push), and the sun was already rising, the other two also finally got their asses out of Emily. We hugged and watched the sunrise together.

## Playa sunrise

I stayed up and welcomed the morning sun twice during the week, the time during the L trip was the second time. The first time (on some other vitamins combo) I was truly shocked - I've been to some of the most breathtaking sunrise spots in the world (and eventually decided that getting up for sunrises is less of my thing), but this one was truly different.

It was for the most hyped Tycho sunrise set, so we were determined to stay out all night. The whole crew was together, until at RoboHeart (an art cart with a heart on the top deck), me, An and Dooley weren't really feeling the music, so we wandered off. We soon found the spot where the art carts were gathering, and confirmed that's where Tycho will be playing. We returned there right around 5am, and only just dozens of burners gathering then. I saw a whimsical looking art cart (maybe an elephant), and said to An that I wish I could go up there - we had tried to go on multiple art carts during the week, and it's always tricky, especially when we have more than one or two people, and we didn't want to be separated. An said let's ask! That made the highlight of the night. We first climbed onto the first floor, and some guy said you should check out the balcony, so we continued climbing. It was a small platform that fit no more than ten people standing, and gave us the vantage point far into deep playa. At some point we started to see two art carts followed by long trails of bikers that looked like tiny ants with lights, following the art carts, that's how we know it's Tycho playing up there. The rhino cart came and parked right next to us, and we had a clear view of Tycho DJing, and the trails of bikers parked their bikes and joined in the middle of the area surrounded by all the art carts, at that point there were thousands of us gathering and dancing to Tycho's set. There were fires coming out of the art carts in the same rhythm of the music, fire artists doing their performances in the middle, it was a party. My vitamins had been long worn off by then, and the fatigue was finally hitting me as the sky was changing color and you can see the beautiful contours of the layers of mountains on the horizon in the background.

It turns out our art cart was located at the perfect angle, looking directly at Tycho's DJ station, and the sun was about to come out right in front of us to Tycho's right. We could tell because that's where it was getting the brightest, and the buildup was getting so exciting. Then when that first beam of sunlight came through, and you started hearing the crowd in the middle start to cheer as they turned to their right. Imagine thousands of people jumping up and down and cheering for the sun - it felt very primal and visceral, in such a touching way. It reminded me of the game in the Three Body Problem, and the kind of relationship that our ancestors had with the sun - worship, respect, fear, etc. I had to leave the group early for the bathroom, and by the time I biked back to find them, the sun was well above the horizon, but you can still stare at its radiating light without blinding yourself. I felt then that the universe gave me permission to embrace life again.

## Connections

Burning Man for me was all about connections, with myself, with An, the only person I knew before the week, with my campmates, and with total stranger burners.

Monday night we met this pineapple themed bar right at the trash fence. We talked to this guy called Will for a long time, he owns a brewery in Austin and he made this delicious tiki punch, and they were serving until they ran out. He and his wife divorced a couple of years ago after she realized she was gay, and he shared how it was a very painful time but he's out on the other end, now dating an amazing woman who he wants to bring to Burning Man next year and said he missed her out there. That was a perfect moment for me to open up, which if you've known me for a while, you'd know that I'm very comfortable at that. Somehow at Burning Man where I went in most excited about the vulnerability and authenticity, it was harder for me to open up, maybe because the environment was just a little bit too harsh and I was always a little bit distracted. Just like during that first sunset on the playa when Radams and I were sitting by ourselves up on the fifth floor of a structure, he shared about his thoughts on marriages and words were so close to my mouth, but the music was a tad bit too loud, and his Australian accent was a tad bit too hard to fully understand, and I just smiled and asked another question back. An helped me share with Will, and I received the first hug from a stranger that week - it felt so genuine and warm, and I tried so hard not to let my tears drop but failed.

During our L trip, Matt, An and I biked to the trash fence, and we found this afternoon high tea setup with two cute tables and chairs, and a woman in a tea apron. She said she was low on water so limiting to one cup per person - that's when I realized, of course she had to drag all the water and the tables and chairs out here all by herself. On one of the tables there was a little sign that said "spill the tea" where she left pens and papers. Matt said "wow there's no better moment" and told An a secret that he had to keep for more than half a year. It was very heart warming. Then later in the camp, when I was drinking the delicious soup that our campmates Ciara and Jason prepared and communicated to us via a cute sharpie message on their van, Matt asked me to tell him a bit about myself, so I opened up too. I live for those moments in my life, and I think the next Burn I'll be able to find my personality earlier in the week.

I think I'll come again next year.
